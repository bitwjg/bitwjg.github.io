Controlled reasoning-efficiency study

Do Larger Reasoning Models Actually Use Tokens More Efficiently?

Starting from an Interview Claim: A Systematic Test with Open-Weight Reasoning Models

July 17, 2026 · Research Notes

The project began with a Yann Dubois interview

This project did not begin with a benchmark table. It began with Yann Dubois's interview on The MAD Podcast with Matt Turck. Dubois, who co-leads OpenAI's post-training frontiers team, made a direct connection between pretrained model scale and reasoning-token efficiency:

“One thing that I do want to highlight when we're talking, for example, with efficiency: if you have larger models, the amount of thinking time—so the amount of tokens they will think for—will usually decrease. And the way that you can think about it is that, metaphorically, the model already thinks through its weights when it generates a certain token. So you can decrease the number of, like, tokens that you need to generate for thinking by kind of, like, increasing the size of the model that you are training. So oftentimes, if you just increase the model size—if you basically pre-train larger models—you will get better efficiency.”
—Yann Dubois, 24:34–25:23 in the original interview. Lightly punctuated from the video's English auto-generated captions; verbal fillers such as um and uh omitted.

This was the passage that gave me the idea for the study. It directly connects three claims: a larger pretrained model, more “thinking” already performed through its weights, and fewer explicit thinking tokens. The mechanism feels intuitive—if more capability is internalized in the parameters, inference may not need to reconstruct the same search through an equally long visible trace.

But intuition is not evidence. This is an interview statement, not an official OpenAI technical report; and “usually decrease” is not a universal claim across every family, task, or budget. A test also cannot simply compare leaderboard scores or treat any shorter output as more efficient: the model may have truncated early or failed to emit a final answer. The controlled question is:

Within the same model family, under matched tasks, output budgets, decoding rules, and repeated-sampling protocols, can the larger model reach equal or better quality with fewer visible thinking tokens?

At the same time, I had been exploring Auto Research: whether frontier models could help formulate hypotheses, design experiments, orchestrate remote runs, audit artifacts, diagnose failures, and converge the evidence into a reproducible report. Yann's claim became a concrete target for that Auto-Research practice.

Public discussions by Noam Brown and Josh McGrath then supplied two missing pieces. Brown stressed that additional test-time compute becomes useful only when the model is capable enough to exploit it. McGrath proposed judging progress on a two-dimensional quality–token plane, where the valuable movement is up and to the left. Together, the three perspectives define the study: Yann supplies the scale–weights efficiency mechanism, Brown the capability precondition, and McGrath the measurement coordinates.

The design follows from that framing: compare two sizes inside each family, fix the task and generation protocol, sweep multiple output budgets, repeat each problem, and admit token-efficiency evidence only after final-validity, token-partition, and truncation gates pass.

GPT-5.5 and GPT-5.6 were research collaborators throughout the project. They helped trace and challenge the hypothesis, design the matrix, audit remote runs, diagnose measurement failures, analyze accepted artifacts, and prepare the report, paper draft, and this article. They are not evidence for the conclusion: every empirical claim below comes from logged jobs and validated result files.

From a broad model screen to two controlled families

The project did not begin with two hand-picked winners. The initial candidate pool included GPT-OSS, Qwen3.5 Dense, Ministral-3, Qwen3, and GLM-4.5. We piloted each family, then asked whether it could support an interpretable within-family scale comparison.

Admission contract. A family needed two meaningfully comparable sizes; matched tokenizer, template, parser, and generation protocol; enough task, budget, and repeated-sampling coverage; and final-validity, token-partition, and truncation quality sufficient for trustworthy token measurement.

GPT-OSS and Qwen3.5 Dense survived that screen. Ministral-3, Qwen3, and GLM-4.5 were not removed because their results were inconvenient: they exposed measurement failure, architecture confounding, genuine scale reversal, and inference-budget interaction. The evidence scope was frozen by comparability and measurement validity, not by which family confirmed the hypothesis.

What the final aggregate says

The result is family-conditional, not universal.

GPT-OSS: the clearest positive result

In all five eligible fixed-budget comparisons, GPT-OSS 120B uses fewer thinking tokens than 20B. In four, it is also more accurate. All four quality-matched frontier points show token savings.

  • MATH500: 95.8% → 97.2%, with 50.6% fewer thinking tokens.
  • GPQA-Diamond: 66.9% → 73.1%, with 60.9% fewer thinking tokens.
  • AIME 2025: 74.2% → 83.3%, with 48.5% fewer thinking tokens.

AIME 2024 is the useful counterexample: thinking falls by 50.2%, but accuracy drops from 84.6% to 81.7%. Scale does not dominate every task.

Qwen3.5: positive, but narrower

On MATH500 at 32K, Qwen3.5 27B improves from 93.3% to 98.0% while using 18.7% fewer thinking tokens and 18.7% fewer total tokens than 9B.

AIME accuracy also favors the larger model—70.8% to 79.2% on AIME 2024 and 62.1% to 77.9% on AIME 2025—but those cells fail final-validity or truncation gates. They remain strict-accuracy evidence and are excluded from the uncensored token-efficiency claim.

The parser question

The Qwen3.5 AIME failures initially looked like a parser bug. Artifact inspection showed that they were mainly model behavior. Qwen3.5 preserves a valid thinking/answer partition but often consumes the budget before emitting a terminal answer. The dominant failure is therefore an incomplete reasoning-to-final transition, not a global extraction bug.

That diagnosis matters because blindly rerunning the same configuration would not automatically fix a systematic finalization pattern. We retained strict accuracy, censored token-efficiency claims where required, and avoided selecting only well-finalized traces.

Ministral-3: not merely a parser bug

The official parser correctly handles completed streams; the main AIME failure occurs earlier, when the model spends its reasoning budget without emitting a final answer. MATH500 at 32K also shows a real reversal—7.0% for 14B versus 31.6% for 8B—so parser repair cannot recover the hypothesis. The report describes a 14B→8B→3B pruning-and-distillation cascade and an RL length increase from 32K to 80K after observed truncation. Those details make distillation path and long-reasoning sensitivity plausible clues, not proven causes. The appropriate classification is measurement failure plus a genuine smaller-model win.

Qwen3: successful execution, but scale is not a clean variable

The Qwen3 pilot showed no primary parser failure, but its direction depends on the task: AIME leans toward 235B-A22B, while on MATH500 the 30B-A3B model is both more accurate (94.2% versus 89.8%) and shorter (3.34K versus 4.04K total tokens). The report shows that the pair also differs in active parameters (3B versus 22B), depth, distillation, and post-training; official AIME inference uses a stop-thinking transition and a 38,912-token cap rather than our hard 32K cutoff. This is therefore a valid but confounded pilot, not an identifiable parameter-scale treatment. Future MoE comparisons should control active scale, training recipe, and finalization protocol together.

GLM-4.5: a counterexample plus inference-setting sensitivity

At 32K on AIME, GLM-4.5-Air reaches 64.4% avg@8 versus 55.0% for the full model; valid token partitions rule out a parser explanation. GPQA reverses direction and final validity is low, so the counterexample interacts strongly with output cap and exit-reasoning behavior. The report uses hybrid expert distillation and trains reasoning RL directly at 64K; our 32K cap may amplify the issue, but a seed-0 64K diagnostic still did not restore larger-model dominance. The best classification is a genuine AIME counterexample plus configuration sensitivity, best followed up with targeted budget, template, stop-condition, and forced-finalization ablations.

A reusable diagnostic order

  1. Separate completed execution from eligible measurement.
  2. Unit-test parsers on complete streams and control tasks.
  3. Keep strict accuracy while censoring ineligible token lengths.
  4. Sweep output caps before attributing failure to capability.
  5. Treat active MoE scale and post-training as confounders.
  6. Preserve valid smaller-model wins as counterexamples.

“Excluded from the main result” can mean invalid measurement, a non-identifiable comparison, or a genuinely better smaller model. Those are scientifically different outcomes.

What I believe after the experiment

Within some controlled model families, a larger model can achieve equal or better quality with fewer visible reasoning tokens. The effect is strong for GPT-OSS and present but narrower for Qwen3.5.

A 50.6% reduction in visible reasoning for GPT-OSS MATH500 and an 18.7% reduction for Qwen3.5 MATH500 are large enough to matter operationally. But they are not a universal law of parameter scale.

The more general lesson is methodological: reasoning models should be evaluated on a quality–token frontier with explicit final-validity, token-partition, and truncation gates. Accuracy alone hides whether a model merely thinks longer; token length alone can reward responses that never finish.

Takeaways

  • Larger models can use fewer thinking tokens, but only as a conditional within-family pattern. The evidence is strong for GPT-OSS and directionally consistent but narrower for Qwen3.5; it does not establish a universal law across architectures or training recipes.
  • Reasoning efficiency must combine quality and token use. A shorter response that never reaches a valid final answer is not efficient. Final validity, token partitioning, and truncation are part of the metric—not post-hoc cleanup.
  • “Scale” is meaningful only inside a genuinely comparable family. Architecture, training data, post-training, active MoE scale, and tokenizer all confound the comparison. This study provides observational evidence, not a causal effect of parameter count.
  • Visible reasoning tokens are not total compute. A larger model may generate fewer tokens while still costing more per token in FLOPs, latency, or deployment expense. Operational choices should evaluate those system costs alongside the quality–token frontier.
  • Excluded families still produce useful evidence. Invalid measurement, a confounded comparison, and a genuine smaller-model win are scientifically different outcomes; they should not all be labeled “failed experiments” or filtered away to manufacture a clean law.
  • The evidence still has finite resolution. The frontier uses four discrete budgets, some Qwen3.5 cells are censored, and GPT-OSS high-effort remains an incomplete auxiliary slice. A useful next study would densify the budget sweep, finish the auxiliary slice, and add total-compute measures rather than extrapolate the present trend.

Research scale

Across the two Codex tasks, the validation required approximately 126.2 hours of effective Codex work—about five days and six hours. This counts active execution, tool use, and remote monitoring, while excluding long idle gaps between working sessions.

The final auditable experiment artifacts processed approximately 874 million tokens, including prompts and model generations. That remains a conservative lower bound: some early explorations and failed runs were not consolidated into the ledger, and the figure excludes tokens consumed by the GPT-5.5/GPT-5.6 research agents themselves.

这个项目从 Yann Dubois 的一段访谈开始

这个项目的起点不是一张 benchmark 表，而是我读到的一段访谈。共同领导 OpenAI post-training frontiers 团队的 Yann Dubois 在 The MAD Podcast with Matt Turck 中谈到预训练规模与推理效率时，英文原话是：

“One thing that I do want to highlight when we're talking, for example, with efficiency: if you have larger models, the amount of thinking time—so the amount of tokens they will think for—will usually decrease. And the way that you can think about it is that, metaphorically, the model already thinks through its weights when it generates a certain token. So you can decrease the number of, like, tokens that you need to generate for thinking by kind of, like, increasing the size of the model that you are training. So oftentimes, if you just increase the model size—if you basically pre-train larger models—you will get better efficiency.”
——Yann Dubois，原始英文访谈 24:34–25:23（根据公开视频英文自动字幕校正标点，删去 um、uh 等口语停顿词）

本文译意是：更大的模型通常会用更少的 thinking tokens；模型生成 token 时，已经通过自身权重完成了一部分“思考”，因此扩大模型并预训练更大的模型，往往能提高推理效率。

这正是我当时产生验证想法的一段话。它把三个判断直接连在了一起：更大的预训练模型、已经发生在权重中的“思考”，以及更少的显式 thinking tokens。这个解释直觉上很对——能力如果已经更多地内化进参数，推理时或许就不必用同样长的可见轨迹重新搜索。

但直觉不是证据。这段话来自访谈而不是 OpenAI 的正式技术报告；Dubois 说的也是“通常会减少”，并不等于对所有模型家族、任务和预算都成立。要验证它，不能只比较 leaderboard 分数，也不能把输出更短直接当作更高效：一个模型可能只是更早截断，甚至没有给出最终答案。真正需要问的是：

在同一模型家族内，当任务、输出预算、解码规则和重复采样协议保持一致时，较大模型能否用更少的可见 thinking tokens，达到相同或更高的质量？

刚好我最近在探索 Auto Research：能否让前沿模型参与提出假设、设计实验、编排远端任务、审计产物、诊断失败，再把证据收敛成可复核的报告。于是，我把 Yann 的这个判断变成了这次 Auto Research 的研究目标。

在把直觉变成实验时，Noam Brown 和 Josh McGrath 的公开讨论又补齐了两块：Brown 强调额外的测试时计算只有在模型能力足够时才有价值；McGrath 建议把模型进步画在“质量—token”二维平面上，看它是否真正向左上方移动。三者合在一起，形成了完整的实验逻辑：Yann 提供效率机制的直觉，Brown 给出能力前提，McGrath 给出测量坐标。

实验设计也由此推出：在每个家族内比较两个规模，固定任务和生成协议，扫描多档输出预算，对每道题进行重复采样；只有最终答案有效率、token 分区和截断率都通过预设门禁时，才允许进入 token 效率比较。

GPT-5.5 和 GPT-5.6 作为研究协作者参与了整个流程：追溯和质疑原始假设、设计实验矩阵、审计远端任务、诊断测量失败、分析验收后的产物，以及协助准备实验报告、论文初稿和这篇文章。它们本身不是结论的证据；下面每个实证数字都来自可复核的任务日志和通过验收的结果文件。

从广泛选型到两个正式实验家族

项目并不是从两个“答案正确”的家族开始。最初的候选池包括 GPT-OSS、Qwen3.5 Dense、Ministral-3、Qwen3 和 GLM-4.5。我们先做 pilot，再判断每个家族能否形成可解释的同家族尺度对照。

进入正式矩阵的条件。同家族内至少有两个可比较规模；tokenizer、模板、解析器和生成协议能够匹配；任务、预算和重复采样覆盖足够；final validity、token 分区与截断率允许可靠测量 token。

筛选后，GPT-OSS 和 Qwen3.5 Dense 形成最清楚的受控比较。Ministral-3、Qwen3 和 GLM-4.5 并不是因为结果“不漂亮”才被删除：它们分别暴露了测量失效、架构混杂、真实尺度反转与推理预算交互。证据范围是按可比性与测量有效性冻结的，而不是按哪个家族支持假设来挑选。

GPT-OSS：最清晰的正向结果

在 5 个通过门禁的固定预算比较中，GPT-OSS 120B 的 thinking tokens 全部少于 20B；其中 4 个单元同时具有更高准确率。4 个等质量前沿点也全部显示 token 节省。

  • MATH500：95.8% → 97.2%，thinking tokens 减少 50.6%；
  • GPQA-Diamond：66.9% → 73.1%，thinking tokens 减少 60.9%；
  • AIME 2025：74.2% → 83.3%，thinking tokens 减少 48.5%。

AIME 2024 是一个有价值的反例：thinking tokens 虽然减少了 50.2%，准确率却从 84.6% 降到 81.7%。规模并不会在每个任务上都形成支配。

Qwen3.5：方向一致，但证据更窄

在 MATH500 的 32K 设置下，Qwen3.5 27B 的准确率从 93.3% 提升到 98.0%，同时比 9B 少用 18.7% thinking tokens 和 18.7% total tokens。

AIME 的准确率同样更偏向大模型：AIME 2024 从 70.8% 提升到 79.2%，AIME 2025 从 62.1% 提升到 77.9%。但这些单元没有通过最终答案有效率或截断率门禁，因此仍是有效的严格准确率结果，却不进入未删失的 token 效率结论。

到底是不是解析器的问题？

Qwen3.5 的 AIME 失败一开始看上去很像解析器问题。逐条检查产物后发现，主要原因其实是模型行为。thinking/answer 分区本身有效，但长推理经常耗尽预算，来不及输出终止性的最终答案。主要失败因此是 reasoning-to-final 转换没有完成，而不是全局性的抽取错误。

这个诊断很重要，因为用同一配置大规模补跑，并不会自动解决系统性的收尾行为。因此我们保留严格准确率，在需要时删失 token 效率结论，也不通过只保留“收尾良好”的样本来人为修复数据。

Ministral-3：不是单纯的解析器故障

官方解析器能够正确处理完整生成流；AIME 的主要问题是模型耗尽 reasoning 预算后仍没有输出 final，因此不是单纯的 parser 故障。与此同时，MATH500 32K 上 14B 只有 7.0%，明显低于 8B 的 31.6%，说明还存在真实尺度反转。技术报告显示该家族沿 14B→8B→3B 级联剪枝与蒸馏，并曾因截断把 RL 生成上限从 32K 提高到 80K：这让蒸馏路径和长推理敏感性成为合理线索，但不足以完成因果归因。最终分类是“测量不合格 + 小模型真实胜出”。

Qwen3：运行成功，但“规模”不是干净变量

Qwen3 pilot 没有主要解析器故障，但结果依赖任务：AIME 偏向 235B-A22B，MATH500 却由 30B-A3B 同时赢得准确率（94.2% 对 89.8%）和 total tokens（3.34K 对 4.04K）。技术报告表明两者不仅总参数不同，激活参数（3B 对 22B）、层数、蒸馏和后训练路线也不同；官方 AIME 还使用“停止思考再作答”机制与 38,912 token 上限，而我们的设置是硬 32K。因而这是一组有效但混杂的 pilot，不能把差异归因于单一的“规模”；后续 MoE 对照必须同时控制激活规模、训练配方与收尾协议。

GLM-4.5：真实反例叠加推理配置敏感性

32K AIME 上，GLM-4.5-Air 的 avg@8 为 64.4%，高于完整模型的 55.0%；两者 token 分区有效，因此不能归咎于 parser。GPQA 的方向却相反，而且 final validity 偏低，说明这个反例与预算和退出推理行为存在强交互。技术报告采用 hybrid expert distillation，并直接在 64K 长度上做 reasoning RL；我们的 32K cap 可能放大问题，但 seed0 的 64K 诊断仍未恢复大模型优势。最稳妥的判断是“真实 AIME 反例 + 配置敏感性”，下一步应做预算、模板、终止条件和强制收尾的小规模消融。

一套可复用的诊断顺序

  1. 区分“任务跑完”和“测量合格”；
  2. 用完整生成流和控制任务单测 parser；
  3. 保留严格准确率，同时删失不合格 token 长度；
  4. 扫描多档预算，再判断是否属于能力问题；
  5. 把 MoE 激活规模与后训练视为混杂变量；
  6. 保留有效的小模型胜出，把它当作真实反例。

所以，“没有进入主结果”可能意味着测量无效、因果比较不可识别，或者小模型确实更好。这三种科学结论不能混为一谈。

实验之后，我如何理解这个问题

在某些受控模型家族内，更大的模型可以用更少的可见推理 token 达到相同或更高质量。这个效应在 GPT-OSS 上很强，在 Qwen3.5 上方向一致但证据更窄。

GPT-OSS 在 MATH500 上减少 50.6% 的可见推理，Qwen3.5 减少 18.7%，都具有实际意义。但它们仍然不是“参数规模越大，token 效率越高”的普适定律。

更一般的方法论结论是：推理模型应该在一条质量—token 前沿上被评估，并明确设置最终答案有效率、token 分区有效率和截断率门禁。只看准确率，会掩盖模型是否只是想得更久；只看 token 长度，又可能错误奖励那些根本没有完成回答的响应。

Takeaways：这次实验真正留下了什么

  • 更大模型可以更省 thinking tokens，但这是一条有条件的家族内规律。GPT-OSS 的证据很强，Qwen3.5 的方向一致但范围更窄；它们不能推出跨架构、跨配方的普适规模定律。
  • 推理效率必须同时看质量和 token。更短但没有完成 final 的响应不是高效；final validity、token partition 和截断率不是清洗细节，而是指标本身的一部分。
  • “规模”只有在可比家族内才是有意义的变量。架构、训练数据、后训练方法、MoE 激活规模和 tokenizer 都会混入比较，因此本文给出的是观察性证据，而不是参数量的因果效应。
  • 可见 reasoning tokens 不等于总计算成本。大模型可能生成更少 token，却仍有更高的单 token FLOPs、延迟或部署成本；真实选型应把这些系统指标与质量—token 前沿一起衡量。
  • 失败家族同样提供了可复用结论。测量不合格、因果比较混杂和小模型真实胜出是三种不同结果，不能都归为“实验失败”，更不能通过筛掉反例来制造整齐规律。
  • 当前证据仍有分辨率边界。前沿只采样了四档预算，部分 Qwen3.5 单元经过删失，GPT-OSS high-effort 仍是辅助不完整切片；下一轮更有价值的工作是加密预算、补齐辅助切片并加入总计算指标，而不是把现有趋势直接外推。

这次验证的投入规模

跨前后两个 Codex 任务累计，这次验证约投入 126.2 小时的有效 Codex 工作时间，也就是 5 天 6 小时。这个口径统计实际执行、工具调用和远端监控时间，不把两次工作之间的长空档算作投入。

最终可审计的实验产物共处理约 8.74 亿 tokens，包含 prompt 与评测模型生成内容。它仍是保守下界：部分早期探索、失败任务没有进入统一账本，也不包含 GPT-5.5/GPT-5.6 研究代理自身消耗的 tokens。
